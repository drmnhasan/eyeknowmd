üìò Lesson 1 ‚Äî What Is Pupillary Distance (PD)?

Pupillary Distance (PD) is the distance between the centers of your pupils. It tells the lab exactly where to place the optical centers of your lenses so you see clearly and comfortably.

Why PD matters

If the PD is off by even a few millimetres, you may notice:

  • ‚Ä¢Eye strain or headaches, especially with new glasses.
  • ‚Ä¢Vision that feels ‚Äúoff centre‚Äù or slightly distorted.
  • ‚Ä¢Progressive lenses that feel misaligned or ‚Äúswimmy‚Äù.
  • ‚Ä¢Unwanted prism effect in the lenses.

A correct PD helps line up the optics of the lens with the visual system of the patient, giving sharper and more relaxed vision.

Types of PD

In practice, we talk about two main PDs:

  • ‚Ä¢ Distance PD ‚Äî measured while the patient looks far away. Used for single-vision distance and the distance portion of progressives.
  • ‚Ä¢ Near PD ‚Äî measured for reading (around 40 cm). Usually 2‚Äì3 mm smaller per eye than the distance PD because the eyes converge inwards.

Example: if the distance PD is 64 mm and the near PD is 60 mm, the eyes have moved in by about 2 mm per eye at near.

Quick practice example

A frame has a geometric centre distance (Frame PD) of 70 mm and the patient‚Äôs distance PD is 64 mm.

  • ‚Ä¢Total horizontal decentration = 70 ‚àí 64 = 6 mm.
  • ‚Ä¢Per eye = 6 √∑ 2 = 3 mm inward (nasally) for each lens.

This calculation is fundamental in opticianry and helps ensure the lenses are positioned correctly in front of each eye.

MCQ 1. A patient‚Äôs distance PD is 64 mm and the frame PD is 70 mm. How much decentration is needed and in which direction (per eye)?

Answer: 3 mm inward (nasally) per eye.

Calculation: Total decentration = 70 ‚àí 64 = 6 mm. Per eye = 6 √∑ 2 = 3 mm. Because the frame PD is larger than the patient PD, each OC must be moved inward.

MCQ 2. A wrong PD was used: true PD = 62 mm, glasses made at 66 mm. What symptoms is the patient most likely to report?

Answer: Headache, eye strain and ‚Äúpulled‚Äù or shifted vision, especially with prolonged wear.

Error = 4 mm total (2 mm per eye), creating unwanted prism. The prescription power can be correct, but the mis-centring causes asthenopia.

MCQ 3. For progressive lenses, which PD is used to place the distance reference point?

Answer: The distance monocular PD for each eye.

The near inset for progressives is built into the design. You mark and block using the monocular distance PDs, then the lens design handles the near zone position.

Case histories

Short real-life style scenarios to help you connect theory with patients.

Case 1. Wrong PD in single-vision lenses (headache & strain)

A 34-year-old myope with OD ‚àí3.50 DS, OS ‚àí3.25 DS receives new single-vision distance glasses. True PD = 64 mm but was recorded as 68 mm. Within hours, the patient reports frontal headache, eye strain and slight dizziness while walking.

Teaching point: A 4 mm total PD error (2 mm per eye) produces unwanted horizontal prism. Always verify PD as carefully as you verify sphere, cylinder and axis.

Case 2. Progressive non-adapt due to incorrect fitting height

A patient switching from bifocals to progressives complains that they must lift their chin to see clearly in the distance. PD is correct, Rx is correct, but fitting cross was placed too low.

Teaching point: For progressives, accurate fitting height is critical. If the distance zone is cut off, patients will not adapt even with perfect optics.

Case 3. High astigmatism & off-axis cylinder

A patient with ‚àí1.00 ‚àí2.75 √ó 180 complains that straight lines look tilted through new glasses. The cylinder power is correct, but the finished axis is 10¬∞ off.

Teaching point: In higher cylinder powers, axis errors are very symptomatic. Axis tolerances become tighter as cylinder power increases.
