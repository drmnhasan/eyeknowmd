Lesson 1 — What Is Pupillary Distance (PD)?

Pupillary Distance (PD) is the distance between the centers of your pupils. It tells the lab exactly where to place the optical centers of your lenses so you see clearly and comfortably.

Why PD matters

If the PD is off by even a few millimetres, you may notice:

  • ‚Ä¢Eye strain or headaches, especially with new glasses.
  • ‚Ä¢Vision that feels ‚Äúoff centre‚Äù or slightly distorted.
  • ‚Ä¢Progressive lenses that feel misaligned or ‚Äúswimmy‚Äù.
  • ‚Ä¢Unwanted prism effect in the lenses.

A correct PD helps line up the optics of the lens with the visual system of the patient, giving sharper and more relaxed vision.

Types of PD

In practice, we talk about two main PDs:

  • ‚Ä¢ Distance PD — measured while the patient looks far away. Used for single-vision distance and the distance portion of progressives.
  • ‚Ä¢ Near PD — measured for reading (around 40 cm). Usually 2–3 mm smaller per eye than the distance PD because the eyes converge inwards.

Example: if the distance PD is 64 mm and the near PD is 60 mm, the eyes have moved in by about 2 mm per eye at near.

Quick practice example

A frame has a geometric centre distance (Frame PD) of 70 mm and the patient‚Äôs distance PD is 64 mm.

  • ‚Ä¢Total horizontal decentration = 70 − 64 = 6 mm.
  • ‚Ä¢Per eye = 6 mm ÷ 2 = 3 mm inward (nasally) for each lens.

This kind of calculation is fundamental in opticianry and helps ensure the lenses are positioned correctly in front of each eye.
